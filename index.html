Rocket Science

The Tsiolkovsky rocket equation describes the motion of vehicles that use propellant:

\[ \Delta v = v_e \ln\left(\frac{m_0}{m_f}\right) \]

Where \(\Delta v\) is the change in velocity, \(v_e\) is the exhaust velocity, \(m_0\) is the initial mass, and \(m_f\) is the final mass.

Orbital Mechanics

The vis-viva equation relates the velocity of an orbiting body to its distance from the central body:

\[ v = \sqrt{\mu \left( \frac{2}{r} - \frac{1}{a} \right)} \]

Where \(v\) is the velocity, \(\mu\) is the standard gravitational parameter, \(r\) is the radial distance, and \(a\) is the semi-major axis.

Differential Equations in Trajectories

Rocket trajectories can be modeled using differential equations. For a rocket in a vacuum:

\[ \frac{d^2 \vec{r}}{dt^2} = \frac{\vec{T}}{m(t)} \]

Where \(\vec{r}\) is the position vector, \(\vec{T}\) is the thrust vector, and \(m(t)\) is the time-varying mass.
